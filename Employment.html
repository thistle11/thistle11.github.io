Canadian Employment Overview

Data and Statistics

Statistics Canada (or StatsCan) is the Canadian government agency commissioned with producing statistics to help better understand Canada, its population, resources, economy, society, and culture. Statistics is a federal responsibility in Canada, and Statistics Canada produces statistics for all the provinces as well as the federal government. In addition to conducting about 350 active surveys on virtually all aspects of Canadian life, Statistics Canada undertakes a country-wide census every five years on the first and sixth year of each decade (from Statistics Canada, Wikipedia).

Statistics Canada provides a number of Canadian Income Survey Products. This includes the Canadian Income Survey (CIS)â€”a cross-sectional survey that assesses the income, income sources, and the economic status of individuals and families in Canada. Data from the Labour Force Survey (LFS) is combined with data from the CIS. (from Statistics Canada, Wikipedia).

Income

Median Income

Starting in 1976, StatsCan began collecting income related information from many Canadian households. Related to employment, income is an important incentive within the labour market to promote ideals such as innovation, leadership and personal growth. From a personal financial perspective the overall objective is to maximize income, generally speaking. From a business perspective, employment income is part of labour costs and the overall objective is to reduce income as low as possible without sacrificing quality, again, speaking generally.

As a note, regions that have been left out of the Canadian Income Survey report are Yukon, Northwest Territories and Nunavat, though Canada as a whole has been included. Data collection for this particular survey began in 1976. Data used to conduct analysis throughout this report focus on those employed Canadians that are above age 15, irrespective of sex. Regional Trends

Let's cut out some of the noise and look at a few key areas that are worth comparing. Eastern is comprised of Nova Scotia, New Brunswick, Prince Edward Island and Newfoundland. Central contains Ontario and Quebec while Western is made up of Alberta, Manitoba, Saskatchewan and British Columbia. I've left in Canada as an overall aggregate comparison as well. Summary of Observations

Canadian Employment Overview

The Canadian employment landscape is vast and in a state of continuos adaptation. Variance in regional employment as well as a focus on the sector breakdown reveal changes over time in the workplace, which could be extrapolated in to the future, with further analysis. Though this analysis is admittedly narrow, it would appear as though employment class is consistent over time relative to other factors in Canada, but more analysis would need to be taken to clarify underlying reasons, drivers, and correlations.

For addition insight and depth, further analysis and research can be conducted in the following areas, among others: self employment income, global trends in employment, global trends in income, income sources beyond employment, part-time/full-time work, contract work, employee benefits, etc.
